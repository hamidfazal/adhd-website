Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder

Integrated Clinical Overview for Doctors & Medical Students

Evidence-Based · Comprehensive · Practical

1. Definition

ADHD is a chronic neurodevelopmental disorder characterized by developmentally inappropriate levels of:

Inattention

Difficulty sustaining focus, organizing tasks, following through.

Hyperactivity

Excessive motor activity, restlessness, inability to stay seated.

Impulsivity

Hasty actions without forethought, interrupting, risk-taking.

Functional impairment occurs across:

  • Academic performance
  • Occupational functioning
  • Interpersonal relationships
  • Emotional regulation

DSM-5 Presentations

Presentation Key Features
Predominantly Inattentive Difficulty sustaining attention, disorganization, forgetfulness; less overt hyperactivity
Predominantly Hyperactive-Impulsive Fidgeting, interrupting, impulsive actions; more common in young children
Combined Meets criteria for both inattention and hyperactivity-impulsivity
Adult ADHD: Increasingly recognized. Many "adult-onset" cases likely represent previously unrecognized childhood ADHD, though late-emergent executive dysfunction phenotypes remain debated.

2. Epidemiology

Childhood Prevalence

~5–7% globally

Adult Prevalence

~2.5–4%

Gender Ratio

Male predominance in childhood; female underdiagnosis common (more inattentive phenotype)

Persistence

~60% retain clinically significant symptoms into adulthood

  • Hyperactivity typically declines with age
  • Executive dysfunction and emotional dysregulation persist

3. Neurobiology

Core Neurocircuitry

  • Frontostriatal dysfunction
  • Frontocerebellar network abnormalities
  • Default mode network dysregulation

Key Neurotransmitters

Dopamine

Mesocortical system; reward processing; motivation

Norepinephrine

Prefrontal cortex signaling; attention regulation

Genetics

  • Highly heritable: ~70–80%
  • Polygenic inheritance
  • Associated genes: DAT1, DRD4, SNAP25

Environmental Contributors

Risk Factor Notes
PrematurityNeurodevelopmental vulnerability
Prenatal nicotine/alcoholTeratogenic effects on CNS development
Lead exposureNeurotoxic; dose-dependent risk
Severe psychosocial adversityChronic stress impacts prefrontal development
Traumatic brain injury (TBI)Secondary ADHD phenotype

4. Clinical Presentation

A. Childhood ADHD

Inattention

  • Careless mistakes
  • Poor sustained attention
  • Avoids prolonged mental effort
  • Disorganization
  • Forgetfulness

Hyperactivity

  • Fidgeting
  • Climbing/running excessively
  • Inability to remain seated

Impulsivity

  • Blurting out answers
  • Interrupting
  • Risk-taking behavior
School manifestations: Incomplete assignments, disruptive behavior, academic underachievement despite normal intelligence.

B. Adult ADHD

Hyperactivity becomes internalized as "mental restlessness."

Core Symptoms

  • Executive dysfunction
  • Procrastination
  • Poor time management
  • Emotional impulsivity

Functional Impact

  • Relationship instability
  • Occupational inconsistency
  • Chronic overwhelm
  • Low frustration tolerance

Associated Features

  • Rejection sensitivity
  • Emotional dysregulation
  • Substance misuse (masking)
Hidden presentations: "High achiever with chaos," chronic burnout, repeated career changes. These patients often compensate with high IQ but suffer internally.

5. Differential Diagnosis

Disorder Distinguishing Features
Anxiety disorders Concentration impaired by worry/rumination; symptoms worsen with stress
Major depression Episodic cognitive slowing; anhedonia; mood-congruent symptoms
Bipolar disorder Episodic decreased need for sleep, euphoria, grandiosity
Borderline personality Abandonment sensitivity + unstable identity + self-harm
Autism spectrum Social reciprocity deficits, restricted interests, sensory issues
Learning disorders Domain-specific impairment (e.g., dyslexia, dyscalculia)
Sleep disorders Daytime inattention secondary to fatigue/sleep deprivation
Substance use Stimulant/cannabis effects; temporal relationship to use
Trauma/PTSD Hypervigilance mimicking ADHD; trauma history; flashbacks

6. Comorbidities

Very common in both children and adults. Always screen systematically.

Children & Adolescents

  • Anxiety disorders
  • Depression
  • Oppositional defiant disorder
  • Conduct disorder
  • Substance use disorders
  • Learning disorders
  • Tic disorders
  • Autism spectrum disorder

Adults

  • Bipolar spectrum disorders
  • Personality disorders
  • Obesity
  • Sleep disorders
  • Substance use disorders
  • Anxiety & depression

7. Diagnostic Approach

Diagnosis remains clinical. There is no definitive laboratory or imaging test for ADHD.

DSM-5 Essentials

  • Symptoms present before age 12
  • Present in ≥2 settings (e.g., home and school/work)
  • Cause clinically significant impairment
  • Not better explained by another mental disorder

Assessment Tools

Population Tool Type
Children Vanderbilt Assessment Scales Parent & teacher rating
Conners Rating Scales Comprehensive behavioral
SNAP-IV DSM-IV symptom checklist
Adults ASRS v1.1 (WHO) Self-report screening
DIVA-5 Interview Structured diagnostic interview
Collateral history is crucial. Especially for adults, corroboration from family members or old school records significantly improves diagnostic accuracy.

8. Principles of Treatment

Management should be:

Multimodal

Combine pharmacological and non-pharmacological interventions

Longitudinal

ADHD is chronic; treatment needs evolve across the lifespan

Individualized

Tailor to symptom profile, comorbidities, and patient preferences

Treatment Pillars

  • Psychoeducation — for patient and family
  • Behavioral/environmental interventions
  • Pharmacotherapy
  • Family/school/work support
  • Comorbidity management

9–11. Pharmacological Treatment

First-line agents: Stimulants (methylphenidate and amphetamine groups). Largest effect size among psychiatric medications. Response rate ~70%; ~85% after sequential trials.

10A. Methylphenidate

Mechanism

DAT and NET inhibition → ↑ dopamine/norepinephrine in PFC

Formulations

IR (3–4h), ER (8–12h), OROS osmotic release

Clinical Pearl

Often preferred initially in children; lower abuse potential than amphetamines

Adverse Effects

Common: Appetite suppression, insomnia, irritability, tachycardia, headache, abdominal pain

Rare: Psychosis, tics worsening, priapism

Growth: Mild growth attenuation in children — monitor height/weight

10B. Amphetamine Preparations

Examples

  • Mixed amphetamine salts
  • Lisdexamfetamine (prodrug)
  • Dextroamphetamine

Mechanism

Promote dopamine release, reverse transporter flux, VMAT2 interaction

Profile

Slightly stronger efficacy, slightly more adverse effects. Useful for adults and severe executive dysfunction.

Lisdexamfetamine: Prodrug with smoother pharmacokinetics and lower abuse liability. Often preferred in adults.

11A. Atomoxetine (Non-Stimulant)

Advantages

  • Not a controlled substance
  • Useful when anxiety is prominent
  • Useful in tic disorders
  • Lower abuse risk

Disadvantages

  • Delayed onset (2–6 weeks)
  • Lower efficacy than stimulants
  • Nausea, fatigue, sexual dysfunction
Black box warning: Increased suicidal ideation in youth. Monitor closely during initiation.

11B. Alpha-2 Agonists (Guanfacine & Clonidine)

Mechanism: Strengthen prefrontal cortical regulation.

Best for: Hyperactivity, impulsivity, aggression, sleep problems, tics.

Do not stop abruptly — risk of rebound hypertension. Taper gradually.

11C. Other Off-Label Options

Agent Useful When Cautions
Bupropion ADHD + depression; nicotine dependence Avoid in seizure disorders, eating disorders
Modafinil Some benefit in adults (not standard) Risk of Stevens-Johnson syndrome (rare)

12. Choosing Medication — Expert Approach

Children

Start: Methylphenidate

If inadequate: Amphetamine class

If anxiety/tics: Atomoxetine or guanfacine

Adults

Preferred: Long-acting stimulants (lisdexamfetamine, OROS methylphenidate)

If substance misuse risk: Non-stimulants

If cardiovascular concern: Atomoxetine or guanfacine

13. ADHD + Comorbidity Pearls

Comorbidity Preferred Approach
Anxiety Treat ADHD cautiously; atomoxetine useful; consider lower stimulant doses
Bipolar disorder Stabilize mood BEFORE stimulants — risk of mania induction
Tic disorder Guanfacine, clonidine, or atomoxetine preferred
Substance use Lisdexamfetamine or non-stimulants; avoid IR stimulants
Depression Bupropion useful for both conditions
Autism Lower doses; expect more side effects; slower titration

14–16. Special Considerations

14. Cardiovascular

  • Obtain cardiac history and family history of sudden death
  • Check BP/pulse before and during treatment
  • ECG: not routinely mandatory, but useful if risk factors present
  • Typical effects: mild HR/BP increase
  • Avoid/monitor carefully with severe arrhythmias, uncontrolled hypertension, structural heart disease

15. Growth & Appetite

Monitor: Weight, height, appetite in children. Growth suppression is usually modest. Management strategies: protein-rich breakfast, evening calorie supplementation, selective medication holidays.

16. Sleep Management

ADHD commonly coexists with delayed sleep phase and insomnia.

  • Optimize stimulant timing (earlier dosing)
  • Sleep hygiene interventions
  • Treat sleep apnea or restless legs if present
  • Sometimes useful: melatonin, guanfacine/clonidine at bedtime

17. ADHD and Substance Use

Untreated ADHD itself increases SUD risk. Appropriate stimulant treatment may reduce long-term SUD risk by improving self-regulation.

Risk mitigation strategies:

  • Use extended-release formulations
  • Implement pill counts and prescription monitoring
  • Avoid IR stimulants in high-risk patients
  • Consider lisdexamfetamine (lower abuse liability)

18. Psychotherapy & Behavioral Interventions

Children

  • Parent management training
  • Classroom interventions
  • Social skills training

Adults

  • CBT for ADHD
  • Executive function coaching
  • Organizational systems training
Key principle: Medication improves "capacity." Skills training improves "performance." Both are necessary for optimal outcomes.

19. Nutritional & Integrative Perspectives

Evidence quality varies greatly. These are adjunctive, not replacement for standard care.

A. Omega-3 Fatty Acids

Most evidence among supplements. Higher EPA formulations (~1–2 g EPA/day) may help modestly. Small effect size — use as adjunct only.

B. Iron

Low ferritin associated with ADHD severity in some children. Consider testing if restless legs, sleep issues, or restricted diet. Treat only confirmed deficiency.

C. Zinc & Magnesium

Possible small benefit in deficient populations. Evidence inconsistent. Magnesium may help sleep/anxiety in deficient individuals.

D. Elimination Diets

Subset of children may respond to artificial food color restriction or few-food elimination diets. Challenges: difficult adherence, nutritional risks. Not first-line.

E. Herbal/Alternative Agents

Ginkgo, Bacopa, Pycnogenol: small studies, inconsistent results. Not guideline-supported monotherapy.

20–21. Neurofeedback & Exercise

20. Neurofeedback

Mixed evidence. Some patients report benefit, but less robust than stimulant treatment. Potential role as adjunctive therapy for motivated families.

21. Exercise

Strong supportive evidence. Benefits executive function, mood, sleep, and emotional regulation. Often under-prescribed clinically. Recommend regular aerobic activity as part of multimodal treatment.

22. Adult ADHD Diagnostic Pitfalls

Reasons for Missed Diagnosis

  • High IQ compensation strategies
  • Internalized hyperactivity
  • Female inattentive presentations
  • Comorbid anxiety/depression masking symptoms

Overdiagnosis Pitfalls

  • Trauma-related executive dysfunction
  • Chronic sleep deprivation
  • Chronic anxiety
  • Excessive digital overstimulation

23. Pregnancy & Lactation

Evidence is evolving. General principles:

  • Weigh maternal functional impairment vs. fetal risk
  • Behavioral interventions preferred if symptoms are mild
  • Methylphenidate: Data relatively reassuring overall
  • Amphetamine: Possible risks include lower birth weight, hypertension
  • Atomoxetine: Limited data available
Clinical practice: Individualized shared decision-making with thorough informed consent.

24. Emerging Concepts

Current ADHD literature increasingly examines:

Clinical Phenotypes

  • Emotional dysregulation
  • Sluggish cognitive tempo
  • Executive function network models

Biological Factors

  • Overlap with autism spectrum
  • Sleep/circadian dysfunction
  • Inflammatory/metabolic contributions

25. Practical Treatment Algorithm

Step 1: Mild ADHD

Psychoeducation → School/work interventions → CBT/coaching → Exercise/sleep optimization

Step 2: Moderate–Severe ADHD

First-line: Stimulant (methylphenidate or amphetamine based on age/comorbidities)

Step 3: Ineffective or Intolerable

Switch stimulant class → Then consider atomoxetine or guanfacine

Step 4: Throughout

Treat comorbidities concurrently → Monitor longitudinally → Adjust as needed

26. Key Clinical Pearls

  • ADHD is highly impairing but highly treatable
  • Stimulants remain the most effective therapy
  • Untreated ADHD carries major academic, occupational, psychiatric, and substance-related risks
  • Adult ADHD is frequently missed — maintain high index of suspicion
  • Emotional dysregulation is clinically important even though not core DSM criteria
  • Integrative approaches may help adjunctively, but evidence quality is much lower than for pharmacotherapy
  • Sleep, exercise, structure, and psychoeducation significantly influence outcomes
  • Always screen for bipolar disorder before prescribing stimulants in emotionally dysregulated adults
